# Personas

Six named personas covering the three sides of the PaddockME marketplace: livestock owners (who need pasture), landowners (who have spare paddocks), and transport operators (who move stock between them).

These are v0.3 — written from first principles for PaddockME rather than ported from an adjacent product. They should still be calibrated against five real customer interviews per type before being treated as load-bearing for product decisions. Dale is the closest to validated (drawn from real regional-customer observation); the other five are best-guess archetypes synthesised from how Australian agistment and stock transport actually work today.

Coverage map:

| Side of the marketplace | Persona(s) |
| --- | --- |
| Livestock owner | Dale (cattle/sheep producer, crisis-driven), Tash (horse owner, continuous-use) |
| Landowner | Brett (active farmer with spare grass), Lyn (semi-retired, paddocks sitting idle) |
| Transport | Wayne (single-truck owner-operator), Sharon (multi-truck family business) |

Dale remains the **anchor persona** — every screen should answer "does Dale's life get easier?" first. The other five widen the lens so we don't accidentally build a product that only serves the cattle-in-a-drought use case.

---

## 1. Dale — the regional cattle/sheep producer

- **Age:** 40s to 70s
- **Location:** on or near a rural property; nearest town 30–90 min away
- **Household:** runs a small-to-mid family farm (or manages one for a family trust); income $70k–$200k; fleet of 2 utes + 1–2 trailers + quad bike + older Land Cruiser; shops at Mitre 10, Elders, Nutrien Ag Solutions, CRT, ProAg
- **Livestock context:** owns or manages cattle, sheep, or a mix. Has paddocks. Knows what good pasture looks like. Treats every storm warning and BoM long-range outlook as a personal logistics event. Has done agistment by phone and saleyard-handshake for 30 years. Uses agistment **reactively** — when his country runs out of grass and he needs to move stock somewhere else fast.
- **What he needs:** a way to find spare paddock space outside his own district when he's dry, without 14 phone calls; written agreement with rates, duration, feed, water, and fencing in one place; a reliable way to find a stock-crate driver going his way on the right day; a record of where his livestock are and how they're going.
- **Friction he hits today:** verbal agreements that lead to disputes about rates, feed, water, and how long the stock will be there; transport is a separate ring-around; no central record of where the livestock are or how they're going; storms and weather windows are unforgiving; the people he'd normally ring (neighbours, mates two districts over) are dry too in a regional drought.

## 2. Tash — the off-farm horse owner

- **Age:** mid-30s to early 50s
- **Location:** regional town or city outskirts (Bathurst, Mudgee, Toowoomba, Geelong, Wagga, the Hunter, Sunshine Coast hinterland)
- **Household:** salaried professional or small-business owner earning $90k–$160k; partner ± school-aged kids; lives in town on a suburban block; owns 1–3 horses (eventing, dressage, pleasure, retired). Pony Club, EA, ARC member.
- **Livestock context:** keeps horses on agistment because she doesn't own land. Uses agistment as her **everyday operating mode**, not a crisis response — this is the high-frequency, lower-volume side of the marketplace. Often the agistment property is just been sold, or the owner is changing the terms, or the grass is failing, and she needs to move 1–3 horses 20–80 km without it becoming a part-time job.
- **What she needs:** agistment within 45 minutes of home; safe horse-appropriate fencing (no barbed wire, no exposed star pickets); shelter or trees; reliable water; clear written terms about what's included (hay top-up in summer? rugs on/off? worming schedule? vet access?); a landowner who'll check on the horses mid-week and message her if something's off; monthly billing she can pay without an awkward conversation.
- **Friction she hits today:** finds agistment through Facebook groups ("[Region] Horse Agistment / Floats / Tack") that are noisy, unmoderated, and ghosted; phone tag with prospective landowners; no written agreement on what's included so expectations drift; when a property is sold she scrambles for weeks and sometimes pays for two places at once during the transition; transport for a horse float is a different problem set (often DIY) but a long-distance horse move is the same problem as a stock move.

## 3. Brett — the active farmer with spare paddocks

- **Age:** 50s to 60s
- **Location:** mixed-farming district — Riverina, Liverpool Plains, Darling Downs, southern WA Wheatbelt, northern Vic, central-west NSW
- **Property:** 800–3,000 ha; runs his own cattle and/or sheep; carries ~2,000 head in a normal year; third-generation operator
- **Household:** wife runs the books, is on the CWA committee, and does most of the agistment "vibes check" by phone; two adult kids — one's an agronomist in town, the other left for the mines and isn't coming back
- **Livestock context:** has paddocks he could agist out 6–8 months of the year when his own season is kind. Has been doing the occasional handshake deal with neighbours and cousins for decades, but it's ad hoc — he doesn't think of himself as "in the agistment business." Cautious about taking unknown stock: biosecurity (Johne's, footrot, lice), fence pressure, water competition, and payment risk are all real concerns.
- **What he needs:** vetted livestock owners with a known property-of-origin and current NVDs; a written agreement covering rate, duration, feed expectations, water, vet access, what happens if it rains or doesn't, what happens if a beast goes down; transparent payment — ideally paid before the truck arrives or held in escrow; a way to advertise spare capacity to the right people without 30 tyre-kickers ringing on a Saturday night.
- **Friction he hits today:** word-of-mouth is too narrow when the season is good for him but bad somewhere distant — the people who need agistment aren't in his network; doesn't trust generic online classifieds; doesn't want to spend his evenings managing disputes; the biosecurity admin is a real and growing fear (one bad incoming mob and his whole operation is compromised).

## 4. Lyn — the semi-retired landowner with idle paddocks

- **Age:** 60s to 70s
- **Location:** regional Australia, on a property she and her late husband bought 30+ years ago; nearest small town 15 min away, regional centre 90 min
- **Property:** 200–600 ha; sold the breeding herd 4 years ago after her husband's health declined; paddocks have been mostly empty since
- **Household:** widowed, or partner is in aged care; adult kids in the city visit every few months but aren't coming back to run the place; wants to hold the land (it's the family farm) but it needs to start paying its rates and fencing bills
- **Livestock context:** comfortable around cattle and sheep — grew up with them, ran them with her husband — but doesn't want to manage stock day-to-day at this stage of life. Agistment is the natural fit: someone else's stock keeps the grass down, the fences honest, and the land producing income. Often the **highest-quality agistment offering on the market** because the country has been spelled for years.
- **What she needs:** long-term, predictable agistment relationships with reliable livestock owners (one mob in for 6 months, not five mobs in for a fortnight each); minimal day-to-day involvement; clear written terms so she's not negotiating from scratch each season; a single trusted point of contact when stock arrives; help understanding what the market rate per head per week actually is; an easy way to do this without sliding into "running the farm" again.
- **Friction she hits today:** gets approached informally by neighbours' kids and cousins but feels awkward negotiating; doesn't know current market rates and worries about being taken advantage of; her late husband used to handle this kind of thing on the phone; the alternative (selling) feels like giving up, but the status quo (paddocks idle) is unsustainable.

## 5. Wayne — the owner-operator stock transporter

- **Age:** late 30s to early 60s
- **Location:** regional hub town — Tamworth, Wagga, Toowoomba, Mount Gambier, Geraldton, Rockhampton, Goulburn
- **Vehicle:** one or two prime movers (Kenworth, Western Star, Volvo FH) + double-decker stock crates; runs B-doubles or triples on long-haul, single rigid for shorter local work; trucks are his largest single asset and the source of his identity
- **Household:** owner-operator under his own ABN; partner does the bookings, invoicing, BAS, and the LBCA / TruckSafe paperwork; sometimes a second driver in busy season; income highly seasonal
- **Livestock context:** moves cattle, sheep, and occasionally horses across his state and into the next. Booked direct by producers, by saleyards, and by the occasional feedlot. Busy in the shoulder seasons (autumn restock, spring marketing) and quiet in midwinter and high summer. **Empty backloads are the structural pain point of the business** — every kilometre run empty is profit deleted.
- **What he needs:** forward visibility on stock movements in the regions he runs — even a week of warning helps him plan a backload; a way to be matched with cargo going where he's already going, not just where dispatchers happen to ring him; transparent pricing per head / per deck / per km; secure payment (he's worn enough slow-payers to know what 90 days does to cash flow); consignment notes, NVDs, journey plans and audit records handled digitally so his partner isn't re-typing them three times.
- **Friction he hits today:** rings around regional saleyards and producer contacts to find a backload; relies on 20-year relationships with a handful of regular clients, which is great until they retire; empty kilometres are the difference between a good month and a bad month; paperwork is double- and triple-handled across waybills, NVDs, LPA records, and journey plans; matching a load to his run is opportunistic, not systematic.

## 6. Sharon — the multi-truck stock transport business

- **Age:** late 40s to early 60s
- **Location:** regional hub town with a depot — Tamworth, Wagga, Toowoomba, Goondiwindi, Forbes, Mount Gambier, Charters Towers; the depot has a workshop, fuel bowsers, a washdown bay, and a small office out the back
- **Business:** family-owned stock transport company — 8 to 20 prime movers running B-doubles, B-triples (in NSW/QLD), and road trains (north of the line); 12–30 sets of stock crates; turns over $5M–$25M a year; LBCA accredited, TruckSafe, NHVAS mass-management module; LPA-compliant paperwork. Second- or third-generation operation — the surname is on the doors.
- **Household / decision-making:** Sharon runs the office, dispatch, and bookings; her husband (or brother, or business partner) runs operations and the workshop; one or two adult kids drive; the rest are employee drivers on enterprise agreements or sub-contracted owner-drivers. **She is the day-to-day decision-maker for platform adoption** — if PaddockME doesn't make her Monday morning easier, the business won't use it, no matter what the boss thinks.
- **Livestock context:** the business moves cattle, sheep, and feeder calves across multiple states. Mix of standing weekly runs (producer to feedlot, feedlot to abattoir, station to live-export staging yard) and seasonal peaks (autumn restock, spring marketing, post-drought destocking). Books loads through direct producer relationships, saleyards, feedlots, live-export agents, and a network of mate-businesses who swap legs. **The business succeeds or fails on fleet utilisation** — every empty leg across 15 trucks compounds into real money lost.
- **What she needs:** fleet-level visibility on available capacity and forward bookings (which truck is where, when, with what crate configuration); load-matching across the whole fleet, not truck-by-truck; backload visibility for every leg, not just the outbound run; driver allocation that respects fatigue rules, livestock time-on-truck limits (the 36-hour rule, mandatory spell provisions), and award conditions; digital consignment notes, NVDs, journey plans, and mass declarations she can pull for a Heavy Vehicle National Law audit without raiding a filing cabinet; transparent rate-card and contract pricing; reliable on-time settlement to sub-contracted owner-drivers (slow-pay them and they walk to a competitor within a week).
- **Friction she hits today:** dispatch is phone-and-spreadsheet — every job is a call, a text confirmation, then a manual entry in three different places; backloads are opportunistic, not systematic ("ring Doug, see what's in Roma Tuesday"); driver allocation lives on a whiteboard in her office; paperwork is duplicated across LBCA records, NHVAS, LPA, and the company's own files; producers expect her to know where every load is in real time, but GPS data lives in two or three telematics systems that don't talk to each other; recruiting and retaining drivers is the existential threat to the business, and **admin friction (paper waybills, midnight texts, missing NVDs) is a top-three reason drivers leave for a competitor**.

### Wayne and Sharon — same app, profile carries the difference

Both Wayne and Sharon use the same PaddockME app, the same UI language, the same tap-don't-type cognitive easing. The difference between a single-truck owner-operator and a multi-truck family business does **not** get its own product surface or its own console — it lives in the **profile**.

| | Wayne | Sharon |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Profile carries | One driver (himself), one truck, one set of licences and LBCA/TruckSafe accreditations, his bank details | A fleet of vehicles, a roster of drivers (employees and sub-contractors), accreditations (LBCA, TruckSafe, NHVAS mass-management), per-driver licences and fatigue records, sub-contractor settlement details |
| Pain shape | "Where's my next backload?" | "How do I keep 15 trucks loaded both ways?" |
| Cognitive load in the app | Tap, don't type. | Tap, don't type. The fleet/driver/document complexity is loaded into the profile **once**, not re-typed every job. |
| What they see in the marketplace | One transport job that fits the next time window | Multiple transport jobs that can be routed across the fleet — same matching engine, just more of it |

### Coordination shape: a 2-party workspace + a separate 3-party transport room

PaddockME splits coordination into two distinct surfaces. Each has a hard shape, and they don't blur into each other.

**1. The agistment workspace — 2 parties (livestock owner ↔ landowner).**

This is where the agreement, artefacts, checklist, and terms live alongside the live conversation. It's the contract surface. Desktop is split-screen; mobile is tabs.

```
Workspace — desktop split-screen
┌───────────────────────────────┬──────────────────────┐
│  Agreement / artefacts /      │  Live chat           │
│  checklist / terms            │                      │
└───────────────────────────────┴──────────────────────┘
        Farmer A   ←── agreement ──→   Farmer B
       (livestock)                      (paddock)
```

```
Workspace — mobile
┌─────────────────────────────────┐
│  [ Agreement ]  [ Chat ]        │  tabs
├─────────────────────────────────┤
│                                 │
│  (one tab visible at a time)    │
│                                 │
└─────────────────────────────────┘
```

The split is already scaffolded in code: [`SplitWorkspace.tsx`](../src/components/SplitWorkspace.tsx), [`AgreementPanel.tsx`](../src/components/AgreementPanel.tsx), [`ChatPanel.tsx`](../src/components/ChatPanel.tsx).

**2. The transport room — 3 parties (livestock owner + landowner + driver).**

A separate coordination room for the actual stock movement: pickup time, gate access, route, arrival, weight/headcount on/off, the return move when the agistment ends. The agreement doesn't live here; the **movement** does.

```
Transport room — 3 parties
        Farmer A
            │
            ▼
         Driver  ←─ shared room (pickup, route, arrival, return)
            │
            ▼
        Farmer B
```

Both shapes are **hard invariants**:

- The workspace doesn't grow a third party when a driver gets booked — the driver lives in the transport room, not in the agreement.
- The transport room doesn't shrink to two parties if the drive is DIY — if Farmer A drives their own stock to Farmer B, Farmer A occupies both the livestock-owner and driver roles, but the room still has three slots.
- A multi-truck transport business (Sharon) doesn't change either shape. Sharon shows up in the transport room as the single **driver** party, with one truck and one driver from her fleet assigned to **this** room. Same way Wayne does. The room doesn't care whether the driver is the only truck in the business or one of fifteen — that's a profile-level detail.

**Visibility rule — the driver only sees transport information.** The wall between the workspace and the transport room is one-way: agreement detail does not leak into the transport room. The driver sees what they need to move stock — pickup point, drop-off point, head count, stock type, weights, journey plan, mob welfare notes, gate access, and the parties' contact details for coordination. The driver does **not** see the agistment rate, contract terms, agistment duration, agreement artefacts, or any other private commercial detail of the underlying agreement between Farmer A and Farmer B. The transport room is a logistics surface; the workspace is a contract surface. Build the privacy boundary in from day one — it's much harder to retrofit later.

Build for both Wayne and Sharon from day one. The marketplace populates the **driver** slot of the transport room; the same UI patterns serve a one-truck and a fifteen-truck operator because their profile carries the difference, not the room.

## How to use this doc

When considering a feature or screen, run it past the three primary questions in order:

1. **Does Dale's life get easier?** If yes, build it. (Dale is the anchor — when in doubt, optimise for him.)
2. **Does it also serve Brett or Lyn on the landowner side, or Wayne or Sharon on the transport side?** The marketplace only works if all three sides show up. Don't build for one side at the cost of another.
3. **Does it serve Tash (off-farm livestock owner)?** This expands the addressable market beyond crisis-driven cattle/sheep producers. It's a secondary lens, not a primary one.

If a feature doesn't serve any of the six, defer it.

## Open follow-ups

- Calibrate each persona against five real customer interviews. Dale is closest to validated; Brett, Lyn, Tash, Wayne, and Sharon are best-guess and will move once we talk to real people.
- Size the Australian agistment market specifically — by head, by hectare, by transit kilometres, by region. Treat the published "agistment market size" figures with caution; most are extrapolated from anecdote.
- Validate whether **lifestyle landowners** (5–50 ha hobby properties, 1–2 horses or alpacas) belong as a sub-persona under Lyn or deserve their own slot. Likely under Lyn for now.
- Define what lives in the **transport profile** vs the **transport room**. Profile carries the slow-changing stuff: fleet, drivers, licences, accreditations (LBCA, TruckSafe, NHVAS), sub-contractor settlement preferences. Room carries the per-job stuff: which truck, which driver, the NVDs, the journey plan, the headcount on/off. Get the line right and the same UI serves Wayne and Sharon without forking the app.
- Elevate the **two-shape rule** (2-party agistment workspace + 3-party transport room) to [PRINCIPLES.md](./PRINCIPLES.md) or a short workspace-architecture doc. Both shapes are hard invariants. Nobody should be tempted to build a "multi-truck workspace" for Sharon, or to merge agreement and transport into one surface.
- Elevate the **driver-visibility rule** alongside the two-shape rule. Drivers see logistics-relevant fields only; agreement pricing and contract terms never appear in the transport room. This is a privacy boundary, not just a UI choice — the data layer needs to enforce it.
